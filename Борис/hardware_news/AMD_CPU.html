AMD Threadripper 9000 “Shimada Peak” is now official

Workstation users and enthusiasts, please come forward.

The Threadripper series is making a return at Computex. Following the release of Ryzen 9000 last year, the Zen 5 architecture is finally entering high-end workstations and enthusiast systems. The official launch is expected in July, about a year after the desktop models.

Officially, there are two subseries again: the WX-Series for workstations, which carries the Threadripper PRO branding, and the non-PRO Threadripper series, also known as the X-Series for HEDT (High-End Desktop). The latter, just like in the previous generation, will not feature the highest core count. However, enthusiasts opting for these processors will still have access to up to 64 cores, as seen in the Threadripper 9980X.

The flagship model is, of course, the Threadripper PRO 9995WX. This SKU is notable for two reasons: it is the highest-end model AMD can fit into its existing naming scheme (bye old Ryzens), and it features 96 Zen 5 cores. Furthermore, it includes 384 MB of L3 cache and a boost clock of up to 5.4 GHz, a frequency spec shared across all Threadripper PRO models.

AMD is also offering 64-core (9985WX), 32-core (9975WX), and 24-core (9965WX) options. Additionally, sTR5-based motherboards (TRX50) will support 16-core and even 12-core CPUs. These lower-core variants are not intended for Cinebench world records but are meant to bring the entire platform—now supporting up to 128 PCIe Gen5 lanes, to a broader range of users.

For HEDT users, the Threadripper 9000 non-PRO series will include 32-core (9970X) and 24-core (9960X) models. Unlike the PRO variants, these will support only 4-channel DDR5-6400 memory, while the PRO series supports 8-channel configurations.
